🔴 The Contrarian


The hybrid retrieval idea is a trap. Everyone reaches for FAISS + BM25 hybrid because it sounds sophisticated, but it doubles your debugging surface during a competition. When your output CSV has a wrong answer, is it the embeddings, the BM25 weights, the fusion score, or the prompt? You won't know. And in the AI judge interview, "I used hybrid retrieval" is a weak answer if you can't explain exactly which queries benefit from semantic vs keyword matching and why.


LangChain is the second trap. It abstracts exactly the things the judge will ask about — retrieval chain logic, prompt construction, chunk selection. You'll spend the interview saying "LangChain handled that" which is a red flag. The evaluation explicitly grades your architectural decisions, not your ability to wire together a framework.


The escalation logic is where most teams will fail silently. Keyword triggers miss paraphrased high-risk cases. LLM-judged risk scoring is non-deterministic unless you pin temperature to 0 and seed properly. If your escalation decisions vary between runs, you fail the reproducibility criterion before the judge even reads your code.


The corpus ingestion strategy is also underspecified. "Scrape the support sites" — when? At submission time? The corpus is your ground truth. If it drifts, your grounding claims collapse. You need a static snapshot with a hash, committed to the repo.


🔵 First Principles Thinker


Strip the problem down: what is actually being evaluated? Not retrieval sophistication. Not framework choice. The judge is checking one thing: can every sentence in your response column be traced to a specific chunk in your corpus? That's grounding. Everything else is secondary.


From that lens, the right architecture is almost obvious. You need: (1) a corpus with chunk IDs, (2) a retrieval step that returns chunk IDs alongside text, (3) a generation prompt that is explicitly instructed to only use the provided chunks and cite them, (4) a post-generation verifier that flags any claim not traceable to a retrieved chunk.


The escalation question resolves similarly. What does escalation actually mean here? It means the agent cannot answer safely from the corpus. So escalation logic = "retrieved chunks have low relevance score AND/OR the issue contains signals that require human judgment (billing disputes, fraud, account access, legal)." That's a two-condition OR gate — not a complex classifier.


The real architectural question nobody is asking: what is your chunk size? Too large and retrieval is imprecise. Too small and you lose context. For support documentation, 200-400 token chunks with 50-token overlap, split at heading boundaries, is the correct answer. This is the decision that most directly affects output quality and it's almost never discussed.


🟢 The Expansionist


Everyone will submit a basic RAG pipeline. The differentiation opportunity is in the log.txt AI fluency transcript — it's graded and almost no competitor will optimize for it. Your prompts in the log should demonstrate: hypothesis-driven iteration ("I tested chunk size 200 vs 400 and measured retrieval precision"), explicit trade-off reasoning ("I considered LangChain but rejected it because the judge interview requires explainability"), and failure-mode documentation ("edge case: company=None with a Visa-sounding issue gets routed to...").


The second opportunity: the justification column. Most teams will treat it as an afterthought. But it's separately scored and it's what the judge reads to understand your agent's reasoning. A justification that says "Matched corpus chunk HR-FAQ-023: 'Candidates can reschedule assessments by...' — issue classified as product_issue, low risk, replied" is a completely different score than "User asked about rescheduling."


Third: the multi-request row edge case. The problem statement says a single row may contain multiple requests. Teams that handle this with a single-pass prompt will produce blended, incoherent responses. A decomposition step — split the issue into sub-requests, resolve each, merge — is a visible architectural win that the judge will notice.

def rrf(bm25_ids, sem_ids, k=60):


scores = {}


for rank, id in enumerate(bm25_ids):


scores[id] = scores.get(id, 0) + 1/(k + rank + 1)


for rank, id in enumerate(sem_ids):


scores[id] = scores.get(id, 0) + 1/(k + rank + 1)


return sorted(scores, key=scores.get, reverse=True)
